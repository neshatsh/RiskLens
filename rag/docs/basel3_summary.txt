BASEL III / BASEL IV REGULATORY FRAMEWORK — RISK MANAGEMENT REFERENCE
RiskLens Regulatory Knowledge Base | Version 1.0

================================================================================
OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Basel III is the third iteration of the Basel Accords, a set of international
banking regulations developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS)
at the Bank for International Settlements (BIS). Following the 2007–2008 Global
Financial Crisis, Basel III was introduced to strengthen capital requirements,
improve risk measurement, and reduce systemic risk in the global banking system.
Basel IV (formally the "Basel III Endgame" or "Finalising Basel III") refers to
the 2017 reforms that complete the post-crisis regulatory overhaul.

================================================================================
THE THREE PILLARS OF BASEL III
================================================================================

PILLAR 1 — MINIMUM CAPITAL REQUIREMENTS
The core quantitative framework. Banks must hold sufficient capital against three
categories of risk: credit risk, market risk, and operational risk. The capital
requirement is expressed as a ratio of risk-weighted assets (RWA).

Capital Adequacy Ratios (minimum thresholds):
  - Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1): 4.5% of RWA
    CET1 is the highest-quality capital: retained earnings + common equity shares.
    It absorbs losses first. The 4.5% minimum is non-negotiable.

  - Tier 1 Capital: 6.0% of RWA
    Tier 1 = CET1 + Additional Tier 1 (AT1, e.g. perpetual bonds with loss absorption).
    Represents going-concern capital — keeps the bank alive during stress.

  - Total Capital (Tier 1 + Tier 2): 8.0% of RWA
    Tier 2 includes subordinated debt, loan loss provisions. Represents gone-concern
    capital — helps absorb losses in resolution/wind-down.

  - Capital Conservation Buffer: +2.5% CET1 above minimum (fully phased in as of 2019)
    When breached, dividend and bonus payments are automatically restricted.
    Effective minimum CET1 = 4.5% + 2.5% = 7.0%.

  - Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB): 0–2.5% CET1, set by national regulators
    Activated in periods of excessive credit growth to build reserves for downturns.

  - Global Systemically Important Bank (G-SIB) surcharge: 1–3.5% additional CET1
    Applied to RBC, TD, Scotiabank, and other systemically important institutions.

PILLAR 2 — SUPERVISORY REVIEW PROCESS (SRP)
Qualitative oversight framework. Regulators (OSFI in Canada, ECB in Europe, Fed/OCC
in the US) conduct the Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) review.
Banks must demonstrate that their internal risk models and capital assessments are
sound. Regulators can impose Pillar 2 add-ons above the Pillar 1 minimums when
internal models are deemed insufficient or when institution-specific risks (e.g.
concentration risk, interest rate risk in the banking book) are not captured by
standardised approaches.

PILLAR 3 — MARKET DISCIPLINE AND PUBLIC DISCLOSURE
Banks must disclose sufficient information to allow external parties (investors,
counterparties, analysts) to assess their risk profile and capital adequacy.
Required disclosures include: capital composition tables, RWA breakdown by risk
category, VaR/CVaR model outputs, liquidity metrics, and leverage ratios.
The transparency requirement is specifically designed to reinforce Pillars 1 and 2
through market pressure — counterparties will price credit risk higher for banks
with opaque or high-risk profiles.

================================================================================
CREDIT RISK — MEASUREMENT APPROACHES
================================================================================

STANDARDISED APPROACH (SA)
Uses externally published credit ratings (Moody's, S&P, Fitch) to assign risk weights.
A corporate bond rated AAA carries 20% risk weight; an unrated corporate gets 100%.
Simple to implement but relies on external ratings which can be slow to react to
deteriorating credit quality (as demonstrated in the 2008 crisis).

INTERNAL RATINGS-BASED APPROACH (IRB)
Banks use internal models to estimate credit risk parameters:
  - PD (Probability of Default): likelihood borrower defaults within 1 year
  - LGD (Loss Given Default): fraction of exposure lost if default occurs
  - EAD (Exposure at Default): total outstanding amount at time of default
  - M (Maturity): effective maturity of the exposure

Foundation IRB (F-IRB): banks estimate PD only; regulator sets LGD and EAD.
Advanced IRB (A-IRB): banks estimate all parameters, subject to model validation.

Expected Loss (EL) = PD × LGD × EAD
Unexpected Loss (UL) — what capital must cover — is derived from the Vasicek model
underlying the Basel credit risk capital formula (asymptotic single risk factor model).

Key Risk Flags:
  - PD > 5%: Watch-list credit
  - PD > 15%: Non-performing credit
  - LGD > 65%: Unsecured/subordinated exposure with high loss severity
  - CET1 ratio approaching 7% (conservation buffer): capital constraint risk

ALTMAN Z-SCORE (PROXY FOR PUBLIC COMPANIES)
Z = 1.2*X1 + 1.4*X2 + 3.3*X3 + 0.6*X4 + 1.0*X5
Where: X1=working capital/assets, X2=retained earnings/assets,
X3=EBIT/assets, X4=market cap/book liabilities, X5=revenue/assets
Z < 1.81: Distress zone | 1.81–2.99: Grey zone | Z > 2.99: Safe zone

================================================================================
MARKET RISK — MEASUREMENT APPROACHES
================================================================================

VALUE AT RISK (VaR)
Under Basel II, VaR was the primary market risk metric. Banks calculated 10-day
99% VaR for the trading book. The capital charge was 3× the average 60-day VaR.
VaR Limitations (known as the "VaR Shortfalls"):
  1. Not sub-additive: VaR of a portfolio can exceed the sum of component VaRs
  2. Ignores tail shape: two distributions with same VaR can have very different tails
  3. Procyclical: models trained on calm periods underestimate risk during stress

EXPECTED SHORTFALL (CVaR) — FRTB REQUIREMENT
The Fundamental Review of the Trading Book (FRTB, Basel IV) replaced VaR with
Expected Shortfall (ES) at 97.5% confidence as the primary market risk metric.
ES = average of all losses exceeding the VaR threshold.
Capital requirement: ES-based model is more conservative in fat-tailed distributions.

FRTB — KEY CHANGES (EFFECTIVE 2025):
  - Move from 99% VaR to 97.5% ES as primary metric
  - More granular trading desk-level model approval
  - Revised Standardised Approach (SA) as fallback/floor
  - P&L Attribution Test: internal model outputs must align with front-office P&L
  - Non-modellable risk factors (NMRFs) get punitive capital add-ons
  - Output floor: internal models cannot produce RWA below 72.5% of SA floor

INTERNAL MODELS APPROACH (IMA):
  - Must pass backtesting: VaR exceptions should not exceed 4 in 250 trading days
  - Stressed ES: ES recalibrated to a 12-month stress period (e.g., 2007-2008)
  - Liquidity-adjusted holding periods for illiquid positions

================================================================================
OPERATIONAL RISK — MEASUREMENT APPROACHES
================================================================================

DEFINITION: Risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes,
people, systems, or from external events (includes legal risk).

Basel III Operational Risk Categories (Basel Event Type Classification):
  1. Internal Fraud
  2. External Fraud (cybercrime, theft, cheque fraud)
  3. Employment Practices and Workplace Safety
  4. Clients, Products, and Business Practices (mis-selling, fiduciary failures)
  5. Damage to Physical Assets
  6. Business Disruption and System Failures
  7. Execution, Delivery, and Process Management

MEASUREMENT APPROACHES (EVOLUTION):
  Basic Indicator Approach (BIA): Capital = 15% × average gross income (3 years)
  Standardised Approach (TSA): Capital = business line specific % × gross income
  Advanced Measurement Approach (AMA): Internal loss data + scenarios + external data
  NEW Standardised Approach (Basel IV SA-OR, effective 2023):
    - Replaces all three above
    - Business Indicator (BI) component × Internal Loss Multiplier (ILM)
    - Incentivises loss data collection: banks with higher historical losses hold more capital

KEY OPERATIONAL RISK METRICS FOR RISK MANAGERS:
  - Annual Loss Expectancy (ALE) = frequency × severity
  - Key Risk Indicators (KRIs): failed transactions, system downtime, staff turnover
  - Scenario Analysis: tail-risk scenarios (e.g., cyber breach, rogue trader)

================================================================================
LIQUIDITY REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

LIQUIDITY COVERAGE RATIO (LCR):
  LCR = High Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) / Net Cash Outflows (30 days) ≥ 100%
  Purpose: Ensures banks can survive a 30-day severe liquidity stress scenario.
  HQLA includes: central bank reserves, sovereign bonds, agency securities.
  Level 1 assets (0% haircut), Level 2A (15% haircut), Level 2B (25-50% haircut).

NET STABLE FUNDING RATIO (NSFR):
  NSFR = Available Stable Funding (ASF) / Required Stable Funding (RSF) ≥ 100%
  Purpose: Encourages funding of long-term assets with stable long-term liabilities.
  Addresses the maturity mismatch that amplified the 2008 crisis.

================================================================================
LEVERAGE RATIO
================================================================================

Basel III Leverage Ratio = Tier 1 Capital / Total Exposure ≥ 3%
A simple, non-risk-based backstop to prevent excessive balance sheet leverage.
Does NOT use risk weights — treats all exposures equally.
G-SIBs face higher leverage ratio requirements (up to 5% in the US for holding companies).

================================================================================
BASEL IV / BASEL III ENDGAME KEY CHANGES
================================================================================

1. OUTPUT FLOOR (72.5%): Internal model RWA cannot be less than 72.5% of SA RWA.
   Prevents arbitrage where sophisticated models produce artificially low capital requirements.
   Most significant change for large Canadian banks: materially increases RWA.

2. REVISED CREDIT RISK SA: Granular risk weights based on exposure type, LTV, credit quality.
   Reduced reliance on external credit ratings.

3. REVISED OPERATIONAL RISK: Single standardised approach replacing BIA/TSA/AMA.

4. FRTB (MARKET RISK): ES replaces VaR; desk-level model approval.

5. CVA RISK: Revised Credit Valuation Adjustment framework for OTC derivatives.

Implementation timeline: Most jurisdictions targeting 2025-2027 for full Basel IV.
OSFI (Canada) issued final Basel III reforms guidance effective Q1 2023.

================================================================================
RISK-WEIGHTED ASSETS (RWA) — THE DENOMINATOR
================================================================================

RWA is not the same as total assets. Risk weights are applied to convert exposures
into risk-adjusted equivalents:
  - Cash / central bank reserves: 0% risk weight
  - OECD sovereign bonds: 0-20% risk weight
  - Residential mortgage: 35% (standardised) or model-estimated
  - Corporate lending: 100% (unrated), 20-150% (rated)
  - Equity positions: 100-250% risk weight
  - Securitisation tranches: up to 1250% (highest risk weight possible)

The three categories of RWA:
  Credit RWA: typically 75-85% of total RWA
  Market RWA: typically 5-15%
  Operational RWA: typically 10-15%
